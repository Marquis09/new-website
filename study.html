Munich Travel Guide: A Web & UX Case Study

A mobile-first, user-centered guide designed to solve fragmented tourist information in Munich.

Project Details

  • My Role: UX Designer, Web Developer
  • Duration: 6 Weeks
  • Tools: Figma, VS Code, HTML, CSS (Bootstrap), JavaScript
  • Project Type: Personal Project / Academic

1. Background

Munich is one of Europe’s most visited cities, yet tourists often rely on scattered blogs, outdated travel sites, and inconsistent recommendations. During my own visit to Munich during Oktoberfest, I experienced this fragmentation firsthand — essential information was spread across multiple sources, many of which were not mobile-friendly.

Core Problem: Tourists waste time switching between unreliable sources instead of enjoying the city.

This project began with a simple question: Why isn’t there a single, reliable, mobile-first guide that consolidates Munich’s history, events, architecture, and nature into one place?

View the Live Munich Website

2. Hypothesis

Hypothesis: Tourists struggle to plan and navigate Munich efficiently because essential information is fragmented across multiple websites, many of which lack mobile optimization.

If this hypothesis is correct, then a centralized, mobile-first guide should:

  • Reduce planning time
  • Improve confidence in trip decisions
  • Increase engagement with local attractions
  • Provide a smoother on-the-go experience

3. Target Audience

Although the site is designed for all visitors, three groups emerged as the most relevant during early research:

  • Sustainability‑minded travelers: Visitors who value cultural preservation and responsible tourism.
  • Event‑focused travelers: Younger visitors attending Oktoberfest or seasonal events who need fast, mobile-friendly information.
  • Families and older tourists: Users who prefer clear navigation, readable text, and structured itineraries.

4. Primary Research

To validate the hypothesis, I conducted informal user research with 10 participants who had previously traveled to Germany or planned to. I asked them:

  • How do you normally plan a trip?
  • What frustrates you about travel websites?
  • What information do you look for first?
  • Do you trust unofficial travel blogs?
  • Would you use a single consolidated guide?

Key Findings

Insight: 8 out of 10 users preferred a single website that consolidates all essential information.
  • Users disliked switching between multiple tabs to compare information.
  • Many travel sites were not mobile-friendly, making them difficult to use on-the-go.
  • Users wanted clear categories (History, Events, Architecture, Nature).
  • Older users struggled with dense text and preferred visual layouts.
  • Younger users wanted quick access to events, nightlife, and transport.

6. Pain Points Identified

  • Information Fragmentation: Users had to jump between multiple websites to find basic details.
  • Poor Mobile Optimization: Many travel sites were cluttered, slow, or unreadable on mobile.
  • Unclear Navigation: Users struggled to find what they needed quickly.
  • Lack of Trustworthy Sources: Blogs and unofficial guides felt unreliable or outdated.

7. Design Process

The design process followed a Discover → Define → Prototype → Test workflow.

Discover

  • Competitive analysis of existing Munich travel sites.
  • User interviews and informal surveys.
  • Identification of core pain points.

Define

  • Creation of sitemap (physical + digital).
  • Development of personas.
  • Moodboard to establish visual direction.
  • Prioritization of mobile-first design.

Prototype

  • Paper wireframes.
  • Figma low-fidelity wireframes.
  • High-fidelity Bootstrap prototype.
  • Iterative layout testing with real content.

Why Bootstrap?

Bootstrap ensured consistent responsiveness, faster prototyping, reliable mobile behavior, and clean, predictable grid layouts.

9. The Final Design & Key Features

The final Munich Travel Guide is a clean, mobile-first platform built using Bootstrap 5. It directly addresses the core pain points identified during research.

Design Philosophy: Reduce friction, improve clarity, and make essential information instantly accessible.

Centralized Information Hub

Navigation is structured around major tourist activities (e.g., historical sites, breweries, museums), allowing users to immediately click into the content they need without external searches.

Mobile-First Accessibility

The entire layout is fully mobile-responsive. Using Bootstrap ensures that text, images, and navigation stack and adapt on smartphones and tablets, catering to tourists on-the-go.

Interactive Search Functionality

A search bar and plans for interactive maps were integrated to help users quickly find nearby attractions, restaurants, and events based on their current location or filtered criteria.

10. Outcomes

The Munich Travel Guide successfully transformed fragmented tourist information into a unified, intuitive platform.

Outcome Summary: Users found information faster, trusted the layout more, and navigated the site with significantly less friction.
  • Centralized Information: Users no longer needed multiple tabs or external sources.
  • Improved Mobile Experience: Bootstrap ensured smooth behavior across devices.
  • Clear Navigation: Categories matched user expectations and reduced cognitive load.
  • Higher Trust: Clean layout and structured content increased perceived reliability.
  • Faster Planning: Users reported they could find information much quicker than on typical travel blogs.

11. Reflection

This project taught me the importance of grounding design decisions in user research rather than assumptions. Although the site is visually simple, its strength lies in clear information architecture, mobile-first responsiveness, user-validated navigation, and consistent layout patterns.

If expanded, I would incorporate real-time event APIs, multilingual support, interactive maps, and more formal usability testing. The Munich Travel Guide demonstrates my ability to combine UX research, IA, prototyping, and front-end development into a cohesive, user-centered product.
